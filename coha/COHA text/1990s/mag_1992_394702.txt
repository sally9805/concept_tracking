For many amateur astronomers , the word " astrophotography " conjures up visions of expensive equipment , hours of trial-and-error attempts , and long sessions at the telescope glued to a guiding eyepiece . But you can capture dramatic photos of the night sky without elaborate equipment . All you need is the photo gear you likely already own . What I am referring to is " nightscape " astrophotography , photographs that combine an earthly landscape with the night sky . Including trees , houses , or recognizable landmarks in the frame provides a familiar setting for the celestial objects . A person in the foreground , perhaps looking through a telescope , places a human presence in the image , allowing the astrophoto to tell a story . Nightscape photography is technically simple  --  it requires only a tripod , some film , sometimes a flash unit , and a camera with a " Bulb " ( B ) or " Time " ( T ) shutter-speed setting . It also requires a touch of creativity . Your choice of a foreground and the lighting for @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ and a great photograph . EXPOSURE TIMES Nightscape photos divide into two types : those that require short exposures ( under 30 seconds ) and those that require long exposures ( one minute to an hour or more ) . Exposures under 30 seconds are short enough to keep the stars looking more or less like dots . With exposures of a minute or more , stars trail into arcs because of the apparent motion of the sky . Both effects produce some interesting photos . For example , attach your camera to a tripod , point it toward the North Star ( or Sigma Octantis for Southern Hemisphere readers ) , set the lens aperture to f/2.8 or f/4 , then open the shutter for fifteen to twenty minutes . If your sky is very dark , an exposure of two hours or more is even better . The result will be a photograph of circular star trails revolving about the celestial pole . By pointing your camera in other directions , you can capture portraits of rising or setting constellations . Nightscape photography does n't require film any more special than what you can buy at your corner drug store . Nevertheless , selecting the right film is an important choice . One of the most common mistakes beginners make is always buying the fastest  --  the highest ISO number  --  film available . For star trail shots , especially from moderately light-polluted sites , lengthy exposures and fast films do n't mix . The glow of city lights will quickly fog ultra-fast film and turn the normally black sky into ghastly shades of orange or green . To avoid skyglow during long exposures , use a film with a moderate speed , somewhere from ISO 100 to 400 . Some of the better films are Kodak 's Ektachrome 100X , Fujichrome 100 , and Kodachrome 200 , and , for extra recording power , Fujichrome 400 or Scotchchrome 400 . These films provide outstanding color reproduction and fine grain for their speed . For shots of the Moon or planets in twilight , you can turn to @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ or Kodachrome 64 . For my nightscape photography , I prefer slide film . With prints , you have to depend on the automatic machine at the local photo lab to select the proper density and color of the print . The result might not be very pleasing Because slide film is inherently more contrasty than print film , it provides brighter , more vivid colors . However , many astrophotographers like to use print films . A primary attraction of these " color negative " films is the availability of ultra-high speed emulsions like Konica 3200 . ISO 1000 to 3200 films are great for capturing the faintest stars possible in exposure times short enough to avoid trailing . Using Konica 3200 from a dark site , for example , you can capture Milky Way star clouds , even with exposures as short as 30 seconds at f/2 . WIDE-ANGLE OR TELEPHOTO ? The majority of 35mm-format cameras come with a 50mm lens as standard . For most nightscape astrophotography , this lens will do just fine . Sometimes , though , a 50mm lens does n't show enough of @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ to a wide-angle lens such as a 35mm , 28mm , or even 20mm . Wide-angle lenses are good for star trails and some Moon and planet shots . But with focal lengths less than 50mm , the image of the Moon is n't large enough to show the Moon 's phase or size . Telephoto lenses , ones with focal lengths of 100mm or longer , are great for other types of astrophotography but have a limited use for nightscapes  --  their field of view is too narrow . However , telephotos can be good for shots of the Moon rising over a distant landscape or a tight grouping of planets . SHEDDING LIGHT ON THE SUBJECT Astronomical subjects come with their lighting already provided . There is little you can do to affect the type or amount of light from the Moon , planets , or stars . But you do have some control over the foreground . Try a number of lighting sources to accent the foreground . On rare occasions , you can use streetlights to advantage . But in most cases streetlights are not an attractive @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ they will make the foreground of the photo appear green or orange . A better light source is the Moon . Because its light is reflected sunlight , it has a natural color balance . The amount of moonlight falling on the ground is also faint enough that it will take fairly long exposures to record it , long enough to allow you to pick up stars as well . Use the Moon when it is less than 45 degrees high in the sky , and place the Moon behind the camera just as you would the Sun during the daytime . You need n't depend on nature or a conveniently placed streetlight to provide your landscape illumination . During exposures of several seconds , you can " paint " an object in the foreground with the light from a bright flashlight . Alternatively , the red lighting used by amateurs and found inside observatories may be enough for shots of telescopes against a starry background . A more controlled source of lighting is an electronic flash unit . You can adjust its location and the amount and color of the @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ @ even add multiple flashes , either by firing the same flash several times from different spots while the shutter is open , or by using several slaved flash units . When using a flash , first choose the exposure needed to record the sky . Then estimate the distance from the camera to the foreground subject . Use the scale provided on the flash to choose the power setting that will provide the right amount of light for the camera-to-subject distance and for the f/stop required by the sky exposure . Unless you are trying to freeze a foreground motion , it does n't matter when during the exposure you set off the flash . By being creative with the composition and lighting , you can create award-winning astrophotos that combine the familiar with the unearthly . And all without expensive telescopes , elaborate accessories , or special films . The secret to good nightscapes lies not in the equipment but in your own imagination .
